Key Expressions

Idiom

Take a Rain Check

A polite way to decline an invitation or offer for now, while suggesting you'd like to do it at a later time.

"I can't attend class next week. Let's do a rain check."
"I will take a rain check."
Useful Phrase

I'm debating between ___ and ___

Use this when you're trying to choose between two options. A natural way to express indecision.

"I'm debating between the train and the airplane."
Time Expression

On the 3rd Wednesday of every month

How to express recurring dates precisely in English. Ordinal number + day + frequency.

"Our team meeting is on the 3rd Wednesday of every month."
Numbers

1/3 — "A third of the price"

Expressing fractions in everyday conversation. Use ordinal numbers: a third, a quarter, a fifth...

"It's a third of the price compared to flying."

Vocabulary

Word

Pollen

Fine powder produced by flowers. A common cause of allergies in spring.

"The pollen count is really high today."
Word

Hives & Rashes

Hives = raised, itchy bumps on skin (allergic reaction).
Rash = red, irritated area on skin.

"I broke out in hives after eating shellfish."
Word

Bluefin (Fish)

Bluefin tuna — a premium fish prized for sushi and sashimi worldwide.

"Bluefin tuna is one of the most expensive fish."
Phrase

Jet Lagged

Feeling tired and disoriented after traveling across time zones.

"I'm still jet lagged from the flight to Barcelona."
Abbreviation

___ Dept.

Dept. = Department. Short form used in business contexts.

"Networking Dept." / "Sales Dept." / "HR Dept."
Word

Baggage Fee

An extra charge airlines add for checked luggage.

"Is there a baggage fee for this flight?"

Conditional

Hypothetical Past (3rd Conditional)

Talking about situations that didn't happen in the past using "If + had + past participle, would have..."

"If I had arrived one day late, I would have been stuck at the airport and it would have been a bad situation."
Structure

"Should have" + Past Participle

Expressing that something was expected but didn't happen as planned.

"The train ride should have taken 3 hours, but it took 5 hours."
Preposition

"For" with Time Periods

Use "for" to indicate the time period a report or task covers.

"I had to write a summary report for last year."
"I had to write a summary report for 2025."

Travel Story: Getting to Madrid

Train vs. Airplane — The Madrid Decision

🚆 Train

  • More flexible schedule
  • Control your own time
  • No long waiting at terminal
  • Expected: 3 hours
✓ Chosen option

✈️ Airplane

  • At least 7 hours waiting
  • Baggage fees
  • Less time flexibility
  • 3-4x more expensive than Korea
✗ Not chosen

What Actually Happened

The Decision: "I had two options to Madrid — one option is by train and the other is by airplane."

The Reasoning: "I can control my time more freely if I use the train."

The Choice: "I decided to take the train rather than the airplane because it was more flexible."

The Twist: "However, the train did not come on time and took longer than expected."

The Result: "The train ride should have taken 3 hours, but it took 5 hours." 😅

Key takeaway: Spain's ticket prices are 3 to 4 times more expensive than Korea's — and the trains don't always run on time!

Key Expressions

Idiom

Up in the Air

When something is uncertain or undecided. The outcome hasn't been determined yet.

"I have to make some project. The project to work with my cowork, but this project up in the air."
"I have to work on a new project with my coworker, but it is up in the air."
Why? "The boss has not decided whether to do the project or not. We are short-staffed and may not meet the client's deadline."
Useful Phrase

Short-staffed

When a team or company doesn't have enough employees to handle the workload.

"We are short-staffed and may not meet the client's deadline."
Request Phrases

Tell me vs. Remind me

Tell me / Inform me = share new information.
Remind me = help me remember something I already know.

"I want you to tell me what I need to do." (new info)
"I want you to remind me what I need to do." (forgotten info)

Vocabulary Deep Dive

Key Word

Tech-Savvy

Someone who has practical knowledge and good judgment with technology.

Savvy refers to having practical knowledge, shrewdness, and good judgment in specific areas. It describes someone who is well-informed, perceptive, and quick-witted — often used to describe someone "in the know."
"I am a programmer, so I am very tech-savvy."
"I am not a programmer, but I am tech-savvy."
tech-savvy media-savvy business-savvy
Synonyms

Fun & Enjoyment

Different ways to say something is enjoyable:

fun interesting entertaining enjoyable
"It is very fun and interesting to do coding with Claude Code."
Word Pair

Coder vs. Developer

Coder = someone who writes code (more casual).
Developer = someone who builds software (more professional).
Programmer = general term for both.

All three are commonly used and interchangeable in everyday conversation.

Grammar Corrections

Sentence Building Practice

Learning to express work situations naturally in English.

Original:

"I have to make some project. The project to work with my cowork, but this project up in the air."

Corrected:

"I have to work on a new project with my coworker, but it is up in the air."

Key fixes:

"make a project" → "work on a project"
"cowork" → "coworker" (person) vs. "cowork" (not standard)
"this project up in the air" → "it is up in the air" (needs verb "is")

Why is it up in the air?
"The boss has not decided whether to do the project or not.
We are short-staffed and may not meet the client's deadline."
